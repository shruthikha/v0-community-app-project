# Nido — Vision

---

## The world today

The village didn't die because people stopped wanting it. It died because the conditions that created it — scarcity, proximity, genuine interdependence — were engineered away over fifty years of globalization, mobility culture, and technology designed for engagement over connection. People moved for work and stopped building roots. Social media replaced the town square with a broadcast channel. Messaging apps multiplied until the signal-to-noise ratio collapsed.

The grief is everywhere, and it shows up the same way whether you're at an HOA in a Costa Rican suburb or a 300-family intentional community: the WhatsApp group that became a complaint feed, the volunteer who built perfect templates nobody used, the assembly where six out of forty residents showed up — off camera — for a six-hour meeting. The people who care most burn out trying to hold community together with tools built for something else. Everyone else checks out. Even communities that began with the explicit intention to do it differently — like Ecovilla San Mateo, where hundreds of families chose to leave ordinary life behind and build something intentional on 494 acres in Costa Rica — discover that motivation alone isn't enough. At scale, the coordination cost of community crushes the community itself: thirty Telegram groups, a hundred Google docs, volunteers burned to nothing, information that exists nowhere and everywhere at once.

---

## The world we're building toward

Five years from now, a small group of Ecovilla residents sits together and looks back. Someone mentions how a callout on the Exchange — a stranger asking for help during their build — connected three men who ended up working on each other's houses for two years and are now among each other's closest friends. A neighbor whose yoga classes started with a handful of residents describes how the platform helped her fill her first sessions, how word spread, how people from the surrounding valley started coming, how she now runs a fully-booked practice and earns her living from it. A contractor mentions the referrals that came through the directory, clients who became friends. A father describes hiking the forest trails with his son using the map, pointing out the lookout his grandfather used to visit. They look at the impact dashboard: thousands of trees planted through coordinated community efforts, Real World School scholarships funded by community-generated revenue, integration scores showing the San Mateo neighborhood well-connected to Ecovilla life.

None of those moments happened because Nido was a great app. They happened because Nido lowered the friction on the human impulse that was already there. The yoga teacher had the motivation; Nido made her first class visible. The construction friends wanted connection; Nido made the match. The platform didn't create the desire — it got out of the way of it.

That scene is playing out across hundreds of communities throughout Central and South America — and eventually North America and beyond. HOAs where the motivated minority finally has tools that meet their motivation. Neighborhoods where the person who wants to know their neighbors can actually find them. Communities of every size and type where the people who care have a platform that measures success not in time spent in the app but in events created, exchanges completed, friendships formed, businesses started, trees planted.

---

## Vision statement

Nido is the platform that turns neighbors into community — giving the motivated people in every neighborhood the tools to build the belonging they came for, and measuring success only in what happens when they close the app.

---

## The North Star image

A contractor at Ecovilla — someone who came to Costa Rica from somewhere else, not knowing anyone — pulls out his phone at a job site and shows an apprentice the exchange listing where he first offered his skills three years ago. Forty-something transactions. A handful of friendships. Work he's proud of. He says he didn't expect any of it when he made the listing. The phone goes back in his pocket. They get back to work. That image — the platform as a quiet footnote in a story that's really about people — is the test for every product decision we make.

---

## Why us, why now

The personal answer first: my wife and I are building our home at Ecovilla because we wanted a tribe — not a neighborhood, not an amenity, but a real community of people to raise a child with, to rely on, to be relied on by. We spent years in a well-maintained HOA of forty houses where we knew two or three neighbors after five years. We weren't looking for a product idea. We were looking for a place to live differently. Nido is the tool we needed and couldn't find.

The professional answer: fifteen years in enterprise IT, product, and consulting — followed by a Series D SaaS company — means I understand what it takes to build something that scales before it looks like it needs to. The multi-tenant architecture wasn't a future refactor; it was day one. Ecovilla is the proving ground: 70 real users already testing, real friction, real feedback. And the timing is right in a way it wasn't five years ago: AI is finally making bilingual-native support viable for a small team, not English with translation bolted on. Post-pandemic grief about isolation is mainstream — people are actively seeking intentional community in numbers not seen in a generation. The tools that exist were all built for property administration, not belonging. The gap is real, and the window is open.

I open my phone and there's a message from someone running a neighborhood association in Medellín I've never spoken to. She says Nido helped her community plant their hundredth tree. She doesn't say anything else. I don't need her to.
